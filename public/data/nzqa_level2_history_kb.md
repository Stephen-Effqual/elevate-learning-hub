# NCEA Level 2 History: A Comprehensive Knowledge Base

**Report Date: 2026-02-23**

## Introduction

This report provides a comprehensive knowledge base for the National Certificate of Educational Achievement (NCEA) Level 2 History curriculum in New Zealand. It is designed to serve as a foundational reference document, detailing the specific requirements of each achievement standard and exploring key historical contexts relevant to the course of study. The information compiled herein is drawn exclusively from official sources, including the New Zealand Qualifications Authority (NZQA), the Ministry for Culture and Heritage's NZHistory.govt.nz, and the Te Ara Encyclopedia of New Zealand.

The NCEA Level 2 History course builds upon foundational historical knowledge, aiming to cultivate advanced skills in critical thinking, source analysis, research, and formal academic writing. Students are challenged to explain historical events using evidence, investigate diverse points of view, and construct coherent arguments. This report is structured to support these learning objectives by providing a detailed breakdown of each of the six primary achievement standards (AS91229 to AS91234), followed by in-depth analysis of historical events and movements that are of profound significance to New Zealanders. The objective is to furnish a reliable and curriculum-aligned resource that clarifies assessment expectations and enriches students' understanding of New Zealand's complex past.

## Analysis of Achievement Standards

This section provides a detailed examination of the six core achievement standards for NCEA Level 2 History. For each standard, the analysis covers its full title, description, credit value, assessment method, specific achievement criteria for Achieved, Merit, and Excellence grades, and guidance derived from official assessment resources.

### AS91229: Carry out an inquiry of an historical event or place that is of significance to New Zealanders

**Standard Title and Description**

The full title of this standard is "Carry out an inquiry of an historical event or place that is of significance to New Zealanders." It is an internally assessed achievement standard worth four credits. The core purpose of AS91229 is to assess a student's ability to independently conduct a formal historical inquiry. This process involves formulating focused research questions, gathering and annotating evidence from a range of sources, and critically evaluating the research process itself. The standard emphasizes the development of practical research skills, including source analysis, organisation of information, and reflective practice, which are fundamental to the discipline of history.

**Assessment Guidelines and Requirements**

Assessment for AS91229 is conducted internally by the school and requires students to produce a portfolio of work that documents their inquiry from inception to conclusion. The process begins with the preparation phase, where a student must identify a suitable topic and articulate its significance in a brief contextual paragraph. Following this, the student must develop specific and focused inquiry questions that will guide their research. The next stage involves a comprehensive search for evidence. Students are required to select and gather information from a variety of sources, which should ideally include both primary and secondary materials. Each piece of selected evidence must be meticulously recorded with full source details to ensure academic integrity. A crucial component of the assessment is the annotation of this evidence, where students comment on the content and its relevance to their inquiry questions. The final requirement is a formal evaluation of the entire inquiry process, where the student reflects on the strengths and weaknesses of their research methodology and suggests potential improvements.

**Achievement Criteria**

To achieve **Achieved**, a student must demonstrate the ability to carry out a structured inquiry. This involves formulating an appropriate focus question and identifying a range of potential sources. The student must select relevant evidence from these sources, organise it logically, and provide annotations that offer general explanations of the content. The evaluation of the inquiry process at this level is typically straightforward, identifying some basic strengths and issues encountered during the research and suggesting simple improvements. The overall inquiry is competent but may lack depth in its analysis and reflection.

To achieve **Merit**, the student must conduct an in-depth inquiry. This requires a higher level of detail and coherence throughout the process. The annotations of evidence must go beyond simple summary, providing detailed explanations of the content's meaning and its direct relevance to the focus questions. Furthermore, the annotations should include considered evaluative comments on the sources themselves, touching upon aspects such as their usefulness, potential bias, the author's perspective, and overall reliability. The evaluation of the inquiry process must be coherent, discussing specific strengths and weaknesses with supporting examples from the student's own research journey. It should consider how these factors impacted the final outcomes of the inquiry.

To achieve **Excellence**, a student must carry out a comprehensive and perceptive inquiry. This grade is distinguished by the level of insight and critical awareness demonstrated. The annotations of evidence must be perceptive, not only explaining the content but also commenting on the deeper insights gained from the evidence. This includes reflecting on the challenges faced by historical actors, the complexities of the event, and the nuanced nature of historical evidence. The evaluation of the inquiry process must also be perceptive, demonstrating a sophisticated understanding of historical practice. The student should reflect on their process as a historian would, discussing the rationale behind source selection, the challenges of corroborating evidence, and the critical importance of considering authorship, intent, and the distinction between primary and secondary sources. This demonstrates a holistic and metacognitive understanding of the historical research method.

### AS91230: Examine an historical event, or place, of significance to New Zealanders

**Standard Title and Description**

The full title for this standard is "Examine an historical event, or place, of significance to New Zealanders." It is an internally assessed achievement standard, designated as History 2.2, and is worth five credits. This standard requires students to demonstrate their understanding of a significant historical event or place by presenting a detailed examination of it. The assessment is typically based on extensive reading and culminates in a written presentation. The focus is on explaining the chosen topic with clarity, supported by relevant historical evidence, and demonstrating an understanding of its significance to New Zealand.

**Assessment Guidelines and Requirements**

As an internal assessment, the specific format can vary between schools but generally involves a formal written report or presentation. Students are expected to engage with a body of historical information and synthesise it into a coherent examination of their chosen topic. The skills assessed include formal writing, the ability to explain historical phenomena using supporting evidence, and the capacity to investigate and articulate different points of view related to the event or place. The selection of a topic is crucial; it must be an event or place that has demonstrable significance to New Zealanders. This significance can be established by showing the event occurred in New Zealand, involved New Zealanders internationally, or had a notable influence on New Zealand society.

**Achievement Criteria**

While specific exemplars for this standard were not available in the provided research, the progression from Achieved to Excellence follows a pattern consistent with other NCEA standards. To gain **Achieved**, a student would be expected to examine the historical event or place by describing its key features and explaining its significance in a structured manner, using some supporting evidence. The examination would be accurate but may not be deeply detailed. For **Merit**, an in-depth examination is required. This implies a more detailed and well-organised response, where the explanation of the event and its significance is supported by a wider range of specific and relevant evidence. The connections between different aspects of the event would be clearly explained. To achieve **Excellence**, a student must provide a comprehensive examination. This involves demonstrating perceptive understanding, offering insights into the complexities and nuances of the event or place. The argument would be well-substantiated with a wide range of detailed and compelling evidence, and the analysis of the event's significance would be sophisticated and insightful.

**Key Concepts and Terminology**

The central concept for this standard is **significance**. Students must understand how to establish and argue for the historical significance of their chosen topic. This involves considering factors such as the importance of the event to people at the time, the number of people affected, the duration of its impact, and its ongoing relevance to New Zealand society. Other key concepts include **evidence**, **explanation**, and **perspective**, as students must use evidence to support their explanations and may need to acknowledge different viewpoints surrounding the event.

### AS91231: Examine sources of an historical event that is of significance to New Zealanders

**Standard Title and Description**

The full title of this standard is "Examine sources of an historical event that is of significance to New Zealanders." This is an externally assessed achievement standard, worth four credits. It is assessed via a formal examination at the end of the academic year. The standard focuses on a student's ability to critically analyse and interpret historical sources related to a specific event. It directly assesses the skills of source comprehension, evaluation, and synthesis, which are cornerstones of historical practice. Students are typically provided with a resource booklet containing a collection of primary and secondary sources and are required to answer questions based on them.

**Assessment Guidelines and Requirements**

The assessment is a written examination that requires both reading and writing skills. Students must carefully read and understand the provided sources and then use them to construct written answers to a series of questions. The examination will assess a student's ability to understand the content of the sources, identify the perspectives of their creators, evaluate their reliability and usefulness, and use them as evidence to explain aspects of the historical event. The event itself must be of significance to New Zealanders, and the topics can range from domestic events to international conflicts involving New Zealand. Past topics have included events like the My Lai massacre and its relevance to New Zealand's context.

**Achievement Criteria**

The criteria for this external assessment are demonstrated in the quality of the written examination answers. For **Achieved**, students must examine the sources by describing their content and identifying some key ideas or perspectives within them. They will be able to use the sources to answer questions about the historical event in a straightforward manner. For **Merit**, students must examine the sources in-depth. This involves providing a more detailed and reasoned analysis, explaining the perspectives of the source creators and making clear links between the evidence in the sources and the historical context. The evaluation of the sources will be more developed. For **Excellence**, students must comprehensively examine the sources. This requires a perceptive and insightful analysis. Students will be able to synthesise information from across multiple sources, critically evaluate their strengths and limitations with sophistication, and use them to construct a nuanced and well-supported argument about the historical event.

**Common Areas of Difficulty**

A common area of difficulty for students is moving beyond simply describing what a source says to critically evaluating it. Students aiming for higher grades must actively question the sources, considering the author's purpose, potential bias, the context of its creation, and how it corroborates or contradicts other available evidence. Another challenge is synthesising information from multiple sources to build a coherent argument, rather than treating each source in isolation.

### AS91232: Interpret different perspectives of people in an historical event that is of significance to New Zealanders

**Standard Title and Description**

The full title of this standard is "Interpret different perspectives of people in an historical event that is of significance to New Zealanders." This is an internally assessed achievement standard, also known as History 2.4, and is worth five credits. The standard requires students to investigate and explain the varied viewpoints, attitudes, and beliefs of different individuals or groups connected to a significant historical event. The focus is on understanding not just what people thought, but why they held those perspectives, and how these views influenced their actions.

**Assessment Guidelines and Requirements**

Assessment for AS91232 is typically conducted through a written assignment or presentation. Students must select a historical event of significance to New Zealand and identify at least two different perspectives related to it. The task is to interpret these perspectives, explaining them clearly and supporting the interpretation with relevant historical evidence. The definition of "perspectives" is broad, encompassing a person's or group's point of view, attitudes, and beliefs, as well as the reasons for them and any resulting actions or responses. The "people" can be individuals or defined groups. The "historical event" can be a specific occurrence like the 1981 Springbok Tour, a broader movement like the women's suffrage movement, or the role of a person within such a context.

**Achievement Criteria**

To gain **Achieved**, a student must interpret different perspectives in a historically accurate manner. This involves explaining the viewpoints of named individuals or groups within their historical context and providing supporting evidence for the interpretation. The explanation will be clear but may not be deeply detailed.

To achieve **Merit**, the student must interpret the different perspectives in-depth. This requires a more thorough and detailed explanation of the viewpoints. The student must demonstrate a clear understanding of the reasons behind the perspectives and support their interpretation with a range of relevant and specific supporting evidence. The analysis will show a greater depth of understanding of the complexities involved.

To achieve **Excellence**, a student must comprehensively interpret the different perspectives. This is characterised by a high level of insight and discernment. The student must not only explain the perspectives in detail but also show a perceptive understanding of the nuances, motivations, and beliefs that shaped them. The interpretation will be supported by significant and highly relevant evidence, and the analysis may explore the wider implications or complexities of these differing viewpoints.

**Key Concepts and Terminology**

The most critical concept is **perspectives**. Students must move beyond simple "for and against" arguments to explore the underlying attitudes, values, and beliefs that inform a point of view. Another key term is **significance**, which must be established for the chosen historical event. The concept of **empathy**, or understanding the past from the point of view of those who lived it, is also central to successfully interpreting different perspectives.

### AS91233: Examine causes and consequences of a significant historical event

**Standard Title and Description**

The full title of this standard is "Examine causes and consequences of a significant historical event." This is an external achievement standard, also known as History 2.5, worth five credits. It is assessed through an end-of-year examination. The standard requires students to explain the factors that led to a significant historical event and the effects that resulted from it. The focus is on demonstrating an understanding of historical causality and the interconnectedness of events over time.

**Assessment Guidelines and Requirements**

The assessment is a formal written examination where students are expected to write an essay examining the causes and consequences of a significant historical event they have studied during the year. The essay must be a meaningful narrative that shows a clear understanding of historical sequence and chronology. Students need to identify and explain both short-term and long-term causes and consequences, supporting their claims with specific and relevant historical evidence. The definition of a "historical event" is broad, encompassing specific events (like the Gallipoli campaign), developments or movements (like the civil rights movement), or the role of an individual within such a context.

**Achievement Criteria**

For **Achieved**, a student must examine the causes and consequences of the event. This involves providing an explanation of the key causes and consequences in a structured narrative, supported by some relevant evidence. The explanation will demonstrate a basic understanding of the links between cause and effect.

For **Achievement with Merit**, the student must examine the causes and consequences in-depth. This requires a reasoned and clear explanation that is supported by detailed evidence. The student will be able to distinguish between different types of causes and consequences (e.g., short-term vs. long-term, social vs. political) and explain the links between them with clarity.

For **Achievement with Excellence**, a student must comprehensively examine the causes and consequences. This involves providing an insightful explanation supported by detailed and well-integrated evidence. An Excellence-level response will demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of historical causality, perhaps by exploring the relative importance of different causes, the interplay between them, or the unintended consequences of the event. The analysis will be nuanced and demonstrate a high level of critical thinking.

**Key Concepts and Terminology**

The core concepts are **cause** and **consequence**. Students should be familiar with related ideas such as short-term and long-term factors, triggers, and the distinction between correlation and causation. The concept of **significance** is also vital, as the chosen event must be demonstrably significant. The ability to construct a historical **narrative** is also a key skill assessed by this standard.

### AS91234: Examine how a significant historical event affected New Zealand society

**Standard Title and Description**

The full title of this standard is "Examine how a significant historical event affected New Zealand society." This is an external achievement standard worth five credits, assessed by an end-of-year examination. This standard requires students to analyse the impacts of a major historical event specifically on New Zealand society. The focus is on tracing the social, political, economic, and/or military effects of the event and explaining how it shaped the nation.

**Assessment Guidelines and Requirements**

The assessment takes the form of an essay written under examination conditions. Students must select a significant historical event they have studied and construct a convincing, evidence-based argument about its effects on New Zealand. A successful response requires a clear structure, typically including an introduction to the event, a description of the historical context, and a detailed analysis of the impacts. These impacts should be examined across various dimensions of society. For example, when studying an event like the Battle of Passchendaele, students might analyse its political impact on New Zealand's relationship with Britain, its economic impact on different social classes, and its social impact on gender roles or Māori-Pākehā relations.

**Achievement Criteria**

To achieve **Achieved**, a student must examine how the event affected New Zealand society by describing the key effects and providing some supporting evidence. The answer will be structured and factually accurate but may lack analytical depth.

To achieve **Merit**, the student must examine in-depth how the event affected New Zealand society. This requires a more detailed and reasoned explanation of the effects, supported by a range of specific evidence. The student will be able to make clear links between the event and the changes it brought about in New Zealand society.

To achieve **Excellence**, a student must comprehensively examine how the event affected New Zealand society. This involves an insightful and perceptive analysis. An Excellence-level response will demonstrate a nuanced understanding of the event's complex and often multifaceted impacts. It may explore the varying effects on different groups within society, analyse the long-term legacy of the event, and integrate the interpretations of historians (historiography) to support and enrich the argument. Incorporating historiography is often a key differentiator for achieving Excellence.

**Common Areas of Difficulty**

A common mistake is to simply describe the historical event itself without focusing sufficiently on its specific effects on New Zealand society. The question requires an analysis of impact, not just a narrative of the event. Another challenge is to provide a multi-faceted analysis, considering a range of social, political, and economic effects, rather than focusing on just one aspect. For top grades, students must move beyond a simple list of effects to an integrated analysis that explains how these different impacts were interconnected.

## Key New Zealand Historical Contexts for Level 2 History

This section delves into major historical events and themes that are central to the NCEA Level 2 History curriculum. These contexts are frequently used as the subject matter for assessments across the various achievement standards.

### The Treaty of Waitangi (Te Tiriti o Waitangi) and its Significance

The Treaty of Waitangi is New Zealand's founding document, an agreement that has profoundly shaped the nation's history, law, and identity. Its ongoing significance makes it a critical topic of study.

**Background and Rationale for the Treaty**

By the late 1830s, New Zealand was experiencing rapid change. The influx of British settlers, traders, and missionaries was increasing, leading to extensive and often unregulated land transactions with Māori. The British government became concerned about the lawlessness of some British subjects and the potential for large-scale, fraudulent land acquisition. Simultaneously, there were fears of annexation by other colonial powers, particularly France. In response, the British Crown decided to seek sovereignty over New Zealand to protect Māori interests, regulate its own subjects, and secure its strategic and commercial position. In 1839, Captain William Hobson was appointed as consul and tasked with negotiating a treaty with Māori chiefs to secure this sovereignty.

**The Drafting, Translation, and Signing Process**

The process of creating the Treaty was remarkably swift. Hobson, with assistance from his secretary James Freeman and the British Resident James Busby, drafted the English text. This draft was then given to missionary Henry Williams and his son Edward for translation into the Māori language on the night of February 4, 1840. This translation, known as Te Tiriti o Waitangi, became the primary document presented to Māori. On February 5, around 500 Māori gathered at Waitangi for a day of intense debate. On February 6, 1840, more than 40 chiefs, led by Hōne Heke, chose to sign Te Tiriti. Following this initial signing, copies of the Treaty were circulated throughout the country for the next seven months. Ultimately, between 530 and 540 rangatira (chiefs), including at least 13 women of high standing, affixed their mark or signature to the document. The vast majority signed the Māori-language version. Despite this process being incomplete, Hobson proclaimed British sovereignty over all of New Zealand on May 21, 1840.

**Divergent Texts: Article-by-Article Analysis**

The most enduring and contentious issue surrounding the Treaty stems from the significant differences in meaning between the English and Māori texts. These discrepancies created fundamentally different expectations between the Crown and Māori signatories.

In **Article One**, the English text states that Māori chiefs "cede to Her Majesty the Queen of England absolutely and without reservation all the rights and powers of Sovereignty." The Māori text, however, states that chiefs give the Queen "te Kawanatanga katoa" – the complete governance or governorship. The word "kāwanatanga" was a new term in the Māori language, derived from the English word "governor." It did not convey the absolute authority implied by "sovereignty." Many Māori signatories likely understood this as granting the Crown the power to govern its own British subjects while they retained their own authority.

In **Article Two**, the English text guarantees to Māori the "full exclusive and undisturbed possession of their Lands and Estates, Forests, Fisheries and other properties." It also grants the Crown the exclusive right of "pre-emption" over land sales. The Māori text, however, guarantees to Māori "te tino rangatiratanga" – often translated as the full chieftainship or absolute authority – over their lands, villages, and "taonga katoa" (all their treasured things). "Tino rangatiratanga" implied the retention of ultimate authority and self-determination, a concept seemingly at odds with the cession of sovereignty in Article One of the English text. Furthermore, the term "taonga" has a much broader meaning than "properties," encompassing intangible treasures such as language, culture, and spiritual beliefs. The pre-emption clause was also poorly translated, leading some Māori to believe they were merely giving the Crown the first offer to buy land, not an exclusive right.

In **Article Three**, both texts are more closely aligned. The Crown promises to extend to Māori its royal protection and impart to them all the "Rights and Privileges of British Subjects." While the translation was accurate, the precise nature of these rights was undefined and would become a source of future conflict.

**Historical Impact and Breaches (1840-1970s)**

In the decades following 1840, the Crown and the colonial government consistently acted upon the English version of the Treaty, progressively marginalising Māori authority. The government viewed the Māori King Movement (Kīngitanga), established in the 1850s to unify iwi and halt land sales, as a direct challenge to Crown sovereignty. This led to the devastating New Zealand Wars of the 1860s and the subsequent confiscation (raupatu) of vast tracts of Māori land as punishment for so-called rebellion. The establishment of the Native Land Court in 1865 further accelerated the alienation of Māori land by converting customary communal title into individual titles that could be more easily sold. By the late 19th century, the Treaty was largely ignored by the settler government. In an 1877 court case, Chief Justice James Prendergast famously declared the Treaty "a simple nullity," a legal fiction with no standing in domestic law. This period saw the widespread loss of Māori land, language, and economic independence.

**The Modern Era: The Waitangi Tribunal and Treaty Principles**

A resurgence of Māori activism and protest from the 1970s onwards brought the Treaty back to the forefront of national consciousness. In response, the government passed the Treaty of Waitangi Act 1975, which established the Waitangi Tribunal. Initially, the Tribunal could only hear claims of Treaty breaches occurring after 1975. However, a landmark amendment in 1985 extended its jurisdiction back to 1840, opening the door for iwi to seek redress for historical grievances. The Tribunal is a permanent commission of inquiry that investigates claims, makes findings on whether the Crown has breached the principles of the Treaty, and recommends forms of redress. This has led to a formal Treaty settlement process, managed by the Office of Treaty Settlements, through which the Crown has provided historical, cultural, and financial redress to many iwi.

Because the Treaty itself is not directly enforceable as law, legislation often refers to the "principles of the Treaty." These principles, such as partnership, active protection, and redress, have been developed over time by the courts and the Waitangi Tribunal to apply the spirit of the 1840 agreement to a contemporary context. They form a crucial bridge between the historical document and its modern application in governance and law.

### The 1981 Springbok Rugby Tour

The 1981 Springbok tour was one of the most divisive events in modern New Zealand history, a period of intense civil conflict that forced the nation to confront deep-seated issues of racism, national identity, and its place in the world.

**Historical Context: Rugby, Apartheid, and New Zealand**

Rugby holds a central place in New Zealand's national identity, and for much of the 20th century, the ultimate contest was against the South African Springboks. However, this sporting relationship was deeply compromised by South Africa's state-enforced system of racial segregation known as apartheid. For decades, the New Zealand Rugby Football Union (NZRFU) complied with South Africa's racist policies by excluding Māori players from All Black teams touring the republic, sparking the "No Maoris, No Tour" protest movement in 1960. Opposition grew, and in 1973, the Labour government of Norman Kirk cancelled a planned Springbok tour of New Zealand. However, in 1976, the new National government, led by Robert Muldoon, allowed the All Blacks to tour South Africa, leading to a boycott of the Montreal Olympics by 25 African nations in protest. This event contributed to the 1977 Gleneagles Agreement, in which Commonwealth leaders, including Muldoon, agreed to discourage sporting contact with South Africa.

**The Decision to Tour and the Political Climate**

Despite the Gleneagles Agreement and growing domestic opposition, the NZRFU invited the Springboks to tour New Zealand in 1981. Prime Minister Muldoon's government refused to intervene, arguing on the principle of "no politics in sport" and that it was the Rugby Union's decision to make. This stance was widely seen as a political calculation to appeal to a conservative, rural, and rugby-loving voter base in an election year. The decision galvanised the anti-tour movement, led by groups like Halt All Racist Tours (HART), who saw the tour as a moral betrayal and an endorsement of apartheid. New Zealand society became deeply polarised, with families, workplaces, and communities split between pro-tour supporters, who defended the right to play rugby, and anti-tour protesters, who argued for human rights.

**The Nature of the Protests and Civil Unrest**

The 56-day tour, from July to September 1981, was met with unprecedented civil disobedience. Over 150,000 people took part in more than 200 demonstrations in 28 different centres. The protests were highly organised and aimed to disrupt the tour at every turn. Protesters marched on grounds, blocked motorways and airports, and engaged in direct confrontation with police and rugby supporters. The scale of the unrest was unlike anything New Zealand had seen in peacetime, with around 1,500 people arrested for protest-related offences.

**Key Events and Confrontations During the Tour**

Several moments during the tour became iconic symbols of the conflict. On July 25 in Hamilton, the game against Waikato was cancelled after hundreds of protesters invaded the pitch, creating a tense standoff with the crowd. This was a major victory for the protest movement. On July 29 in Wellington, police used batons on unarmed protesters for the first time during a march on Molesworth Street, outside Parliament. This event radicalised many protesters, who began wearing helmets and makeshift armour for protection. The final test match at Eden Park in Auckland on September 12 was marred by chaos both inside and outside the ground. While protesters and police clashed violently in the surrounding streets, a low-flying Cessna aircraft circled the stadium, dropping flour bombs, smoke bombs, and leaflets onto the pitch.

**Impact and Lasting Legacy on New Zealand Society**

The 1981 tour left deep scars on New Zealand society but also acted as a catalyst for change. It shattered the myth of New Zealand as a country with perfect race relations, forcing Pākehā to confront issues of racism both abroad and at home. The tour damaged the relationship between the public and the police, whose use of riot squads and long batons was highly controversial. Politically, while Muldoon's National Party narrowly won the 1981 election, the social division and international condemnation contributed to a broader shift in the country's political landscape, culminating in the election of the reformist Labour government in 1984. The tour marked a turning point in New Zealand's national identity, strengthening its image as an independent nation willing to take a moral stand on the world stage and moving it further away from its colonial identity as the "Britain of the South Seas." For many, the tour remains a defining moment in their lives and in the nation's journey toward a more socially conscious and independent identity.

### Other Significant Historical Contexts

While the Treaty and the 1981 Tour are foundational, several other historical contexts are commonly studied at NCEA Level 2, providing rich material for inquiry, source analysis, and essay writing. These include the invasion and occupation of **Parihaka** in 1881, a powerful story of non-violent resistance and state oppression, which is often examined for its differing perspectives (AS91232). The **Battle of Passchendaele** and New Zealand's involvement in the First World War are frequently studied for AS91234, allowing for a deep analysis of how a distant conflict profoundly affected New Zealand's society, economy, politics, and sense of national identity. International events such as the **Russian Revolution** or the **Black Death** are also common topics, often used for AS91233 to examine complex causes and far-reaching consequences. These diverse contexts allow students to apply their historical skills to a wide range of events, developing a broad and comparative understanding of the past.
